  • Definition and Structure: Quaternion algebra is a number system that extends complex numbers, introduced by Sir William Rowan Hamilton in 1843. It consists of elements that can be expressed in the form (q = a + bi + cj + dk), where (a, b, c,) and (d) are real numbers and (i, j, k) are the fundamental quaternion units.

  • Non-commutativity: One of the hallmark features of quaternion algebra is that multiplication is non-commutative; that is, (pq \neq qp) for quaternions (p) and (q). This property is fundamental in various applications, particularly in three-dimensional rotations and computer graphics.

  • Applications: Quaternions are widely used in physics and engineering, particularly in representing 3D rotations, controlling spacecraft orientation, and in computer graphics for smooth interpolations and animations (e.g., in character rigging and motion smoothing).

  • Structure: Quaternions form a real 4D associative, non-commutative algebra with basis {1, i, j, k} and multiplication rules i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1.

  • Key operations: For q = a + bi + cj + dk, the conjugate is q = a - bi - cj - dk, the norm is N(q) = a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2, and the inverse is q^{-1} = q/N(q) for q ≠ 0.

  • Geometry and applications: Unit quaternions (N = 1) represent 3D rotations; rotation is performed by q v q^{-1} for a pure imaginary v (vector). Widely used in computer graphics, robotics, and physics.
